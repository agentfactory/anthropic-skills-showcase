# Verdant Brand Voice Guide
### Version 2.1 — Internal Reference

---

## About Verdant

Verdant makes supplements and functional teas for people who want to support their health without the wellness industry noise. No miracle claims, no guilt, no jargon. Just good ingredients, honest copy, and a brand that respects the intelligence of its customers.

Our customers are 28–45, curious, busy, and deeply sceptical of brands that oversell. They've been burned by hype. They respond to honesty.

---

## Tone Pillars

**Warm** — We write like a knowledgeable friend, not a health authority or a lifestyle coach. We're in the room with you. We care. We're not lecturing.

**Direct** — Say the thing. No hedging, no qualifiers, no "many people find that...". If we believe something, we say it. Short sentences. Active voice. Verb-first where possible.

**Never preachy** — We don't tell people how to live. We don't use guilt as a lever. We never imply that someone is failing and we're the solution. We offer. We don't demand.

---

## Vocabulary Rules

### Use these words
- **nourish / nourishing** — our preferred alternative to "fuel", "consume", "take"
- **steady** — for energy, mood, performance. Not "sustained" or "optimised"
- **genuine** — for authenticity claims. Not "authentic" (overused in the category)
- **depletes / replenishes** — for the body's relationship with nutrients over time
- **notice** — softer than "feel the difference" or "experience results"

### Avoid these words
- **holistic** — borrowed from corporate wellness; feels generic
- **synergy** — meaningless in consumer copy
- **optimize / optimise** — clinical, tech-flavoured
- **wellness journey** — cliche; implies the customer is not yet well
- **supercharge / level up** — hype language; our customers don't trust it
- **game-changer** — overused across every category
- **transform / transformation** — too dramatic; we don't promise miracles
- **fuel** (as a verb for the body) — mechanical; bodies are not engines
- **consume** (for supplements) — use "take" or "nourish with" instead

### Category-specific guidance
- Supplements: "take" not "consume"; "each capsule" not "every dose"
- Teas: "steep" not "brew" (more specific, more intentional)
- Benefits: state them as observations ("many people notice...") not guarantees

## Punctuation & Style Conventions

**Em dashes (—):** Encouraged. Use to add an aside or pivot mid-sentence without breaking flow. Not a replacement for a full stop.

**Oxford comma:** Non-negotiable. Always use the serial comma.

**Exclamation marks:** Maximum one per page or per email. Zero is fine. More than one reads as hype. Never two in a row.

**Ellipsis:** Use sparingly. One per email or article, maximum. Never use to create false urgency ("Act now...").

**Sentence length:** Mix short and medium. Avoid sentences over 25 words. One-word sentences are fine.

**Capitalisation:** Do not capitalise words for emphasis (NO ALL-CAPS IN COPY). Use emphasis through word choice, not visual weight.

**Rhetorical questions:** Fine in moderation. One per section, maximum. Never as a hook if the answer is obvious.

---

## Personality Pillars

**Grounded** — We're not in the clouds. We talk about real things — what depletes you, what supports you, what a hard week actually feels like. Our copy is rooted in the ordinary rhythm of a person's life.

**Curious** — We ask good questions. We don't pretend to have everything figured out. We're interested in the science, the process, the story behind an ingredient. That intellectual curiosity shows up in the copy.

**Quietly confident** — We don't need to shout. We don't need to pile on the proof points. We know the product is good, and we let that confidence come through in understatement rather than amplification.

---

## What Verdant Copy Sounds Like

**Good example (product description):**
> Ashwagandha root, harvested at peak potency, to support what a busy week takes out of you. Take one each evening — or two if it's been that kind of day.

**Good example (social caption):**
> Some days, steady is the whole goal. That's why we make this.

**Good example (email subject):**
> A quieter kind of energy — now in our starter pack

# Off-Brand Copy Samples — For Demo Use Only

These three pieces were submitted by team members. They need to be rewritten to match the Verdant brand voice guide above.

---

## SAMPLE 1 — Blog Intro (Too Formal)

**Original (off-brand):**

> It is widely acknowledged within the scientific and wellness communities that the modern human body is frequently subjected to significant micronutrient depletion as a result of contemporary dietary patterns, environmental stressors, and the accelerating pace of daily life. It has been suggested by numerous researchers that strategic supplementation may serve as a beneficial intervention for individuals seeking to mitigate these effects. Verdant's product range has been formulated with these considerations in mind, incorporating evidence-informed ingredients designed to address the nutritional gaps commonly observed in adult populations.

**What's wrong:**
- Passive voice throughout ("it is widely acknowledged", "it has been suggested")
- Academic register — sounds like a journal abstract, not a brand
- No warmth, no directness
- Uses "supplementation" instead of "take" convention
- Zero personality — reads like a legal disclaimer

---

## SAMPLE 2 — Social Post (Too Hype-y)

**Original (off-brand):**

> READY TO LEVEL UP YOUR WELLNESS JOURNEY?! Our NEW Ashwagandha + B-Complex blend is HERE and it's an absolute GAME-CHANGER! Say goodbye to afternoon crashes and hello to ALL-DAY ENERGY OPTIMIZATION!! This is the synergy your body has been craving! Don't miss out — SHOP NOW and TRANSFORM your daily routine!!! 🚀🚀🚀

**What's wrong:**
- Five exclamation marks (limit is one)
- Multiple all-caps words
- Banned vocabulary: "level up", "wellness journey", "game-changer", "synergy", "optimization", "transform"
- Rocket emojis — not Verdant's visual language
- Urgency tactic ("Don't miss out") that implies scarcity — dishonest
- "All-day energy optimization" — clinical and hype at the same time

---

## SAMPLE 3 — Product Page Headline (Generic)

**Original (off-brand):**

> Premium supplements for modern living.

**What's wrong:**
- Could apply to any supplement brand in existence
- No specific benefit
- No brand personality
- "Modern living" is cliche
- No approved vocabulary used
- Zero curiosity, warmth, or confidence
